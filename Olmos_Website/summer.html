Program Notes

Ludwig van Beethoven (1770-1827)

Trio in C Major, Op. 87

In preparation for his revolutionary symphonies, Ludwig van Beethoven spent the better part of his twenties writing music for a large variety of instrument combinations, including a wide range of wind instruments. Under the employment of the Bonn Court, which tended to an ensemble of wind players, Beethoven practiced composing grammatically for these unique instruments.

This trios' high opus number, which places it near the Seventh Symphony, is merely a publishing incident, the piece is one of Beethoven's earliest! The Trio is ideally suited to skilled non-professional performers, being written for amateur street players. Ironically, while not particularly difficult, it is charmingly melodic and extremely agreeable. Despite being twenty minutes long, the trio is in the four-movement classical symphony form that Beethoven was already exploring and altering during his youth. The first movement is a common Allegro with a pronounced slow introduction that Beethoven was so fond of. The second is a beautiful classically structured Adagio and the third, as is with almost all Beethoven symphonies, a Scherzo. The Finale is marked Presto leaving the three wind players on stage thankful for air.

Ludwig van Beethoven (1770-1827)

An die Ferne Geliebte Op. 9

One of Beethoven’s many accomplishments include composing what is widely considered the first ever song cycle, An Die Ferne Geliebte Op. 98 (To the Distant Beloved). In 1812, four years before the completion of this song cycle, Beethoven wrote an anguished letter to his Immortal Beloved, a woman whose identity still remains unknown. This letter, written on ten small pages, was found only after Beethoven’s death among his many other historic possessions. The theme of separation from the distant beloved looms heavily in this work set to six poems by Aloïs Jitelles. The six songs are sung by a solitary lover seated on a hillside, gazing into the distance and longing for the object of his affection through images of pure, unspoiled nature.

Ludwig van Beethoven (1770-1827)

Trio in Eb Major Op. 38

Throughout Beethoven’s entire repertoire, pairings of instruments that he loved can be clearly found, a notable one being the relationship between clarinet and bassoon. It is thanks to Beethoven, that the two became close musical partners, both in orchestral and chamber music.

The septet, Op. 20, written in 1799 and 1800, helped lift Beethoven’s early career, and its popularity led publishers to release numerous arrangements for varied ensembles.  This particular trio arrangement with clarinet and bassoon with piano was created by Beethoven himself in 1805, five years later. Given that making extra money was a significant incentive for Beethoven, the instruments of this trio arrangement were chosen based on selling the most possible copies!

The work is written in six movements. The first movement begins with an extended prologue marked Adagio building for the momentum of the Allegro con brio. It is thanks to Beethoven's characteristic piano transcribing abilities that the string parts of the septet are exquisitely substituted. The slow movement, adagio cantabile, is a wonderful melodic presentation of the clarinet's lyrical qualities, while bassoon adds its own brand of cheeky wit in the third movement, Tempo di Minuetto. The fourth and fifth movements, Andante con Variazioni and Scherzo resemble the middle movements of a Beethoven symphony. The final movement faithfully substitutes the famous violin cadenza with the piano with a spirited Presto.

Program Notes

Johann Sebastian Bach (1685-1750)

Sonata in G Major, BWV 1027

Born in 1685, Johann Sebastian Bach is often referred to as the father of Western Music. With the colossal volume of compositions written by Bach, every instrument of the Baroque era was fortunate enough to have multiple works composed for it. As is with most of his solo instrumental works, the Sonata in G Major for viola de gamba was written during Bach’s post as cantor of Leipzig’s St. Thomas’ Church in the 1720’s. With his additional appointment as music director of Leipzig’s Collegium Musicum, an educational institute, Bach took advantage of the opportunity to compose a large set of non-liturgical music, primarily instrumental music. The sonata is composed as a proper trio sonata form, with the basso continuo playing a full role in both melodic and contrapuntal dialogue, rather than just accompaniment.

Antonio Vivaldi (1678-1741)

Flute Sonata in d minor, RV 49

Like Bach, Antonio Vivaldi is renowned for his prolific compositions, most of which he composed under the hospices of an all girls’ orphanage in Venice, the Ospedale della Pietà. This lifelong relationship to the institution helps explain Vivaldi’s astounding five hundred concertos and ninety solo sonatas. Vivaldi’s music was viewed as innovative, with many of his compositions stunning audiences with flurries of trills, accents, intentional dissonance, and other extravagant effects. Interestingly, this Flute Sonata in d minor is a little different from much of Vivaldi’s work. It rarely tonicizes a major key, nor does it show off until the very last minute of the work, giving it an unusually sorrowful character. It is worth noting that Vivaldi’s most celebrated pupil was none other than Johann Sebastian Bach.

George Frideric Handel (1685-1759)

Trio Sonata II in D Major Op. 5

Another titan of the Baroque Era, George Frideric Handel was a versatile composer who had the luck of developing his musical talents in three separate countries: Germany, Italy, and England. With so much compositional exposure and an immersive personality, Handel composed a significant library with 42 operas, 25 oratorios, 120 cantatas. The Trio Sonata in D Major is a short, lush, and charming five movement work written in Handel’s native Germany. Being composed in the earlier years of Handel’s career, the slow-fast-slow-fast structure of the work is obvious, but nonetheless cheeky. It is right around the time of composition of the Op. 5 Trio Sonatas that Handel made the decision with his classmate and composer of the next piece on this program, Georg Phillipp Telemann, to drop out of their law studies to become composers.

Georg Phillipp Telemann (1681-1767)

Oboe Sonata in a minor, TWV 41:a3

Telemann’s talent as a multi-instrumentalist was not uncommon in the Baroque era, and this skill is made obvious in the way he writes his woodwinds sonatas. The Oboe Sonata in a minor, while also performed on flute and violin, is full of opportunities for the oboe to demonstrate its unique variety of tone colors and articulations, despite its fairly short playing range. The work is written in standard four movement sonata style. The first movement, Siciliana, full of dotted rhythms typical to the Baroque style, is passionately mournful. The second is a quick allegro, third a restful andante, and the fourth a brisk vivace. Accompanied only by a basso continuo line, the cello and harpsichord players are expected to improvise a moving line above the one note parts they are given.

Johann Gottlieb Goldberg (1727-1756)

Trio Sonata in C Major DürG 13

Johann Gottlieb Goldberg, the probable original performer and name lender to Bach’s renowned Goldberg Variations, is somewhat of a mystery. Little is known about his childhood other than that he was a gifted pupil of Johann Sebastian Bach. The fable goes that upon asking his famous teacher for new late night gig music, Goldberg was responded to with a set of keyboard variations based on an aria that Goldberg, himself, had written. This final work on the program, Trio sonata in C Major DürG 13, was written specifically to imitate Bach’s compositions and was even mistakenly published in 1761 as a work of J.S. Bach himself! Sadly, Goldberg did not live past 29, and so his work catalogue is very limited.

Program Notes

Philippe Gaubert (1879-1941)

Trois Aquarelles for Flûte, Cello, Piano

A flutist himself, Gaubert was a student of Paul Taffanel’s in Paris. Unlike most woodwind-playing composers, Gaubert was also a professional performer, winning top prizes at the Paris Conservatoire and joining the flute section at the orchestra of the Paris Operá. His erudite abilities and work-oriented attitude lead him to become the principal conductor and artistic director of the very same Paris Operá, premiering works of composers such as Gabriel Fauré and Jacques Ibert. Gaubert composed the Aquarelles (watercolors) for flute, cello, and piano while serving in the French army. However, these optimistic pleasant pieces are not a reflection on horror or war. They should simply be interpreted as three paintings, the first an open landscape, second a sun–set, and third a town dance.

Celso Machado (1953-Present)

Musique Populaires Brasiliennes

Celso Machado is a Brazilian world music guitarist and percussionist who for over forty years has performed on concert stages across the world. Having played with renowned guitarists such as Badi Assad, Romero Lubambo, Yamandu Costa, and Solorazaf, Celso Machado was renowned in the Brazilian jazz community. I have chosen four of the six Brasiliennes tunes (Pacoca, Piazza Vittorio, Sambossa, Pe de Moleque) to demonstrate all the strange and unique sounds of other members of the flute family, wonderfully partnered with guitar.

Johann Sebastian Bach (1685-1750)

Sonata in C Major, BWV 1033

Born in 1685, Johann Sebastian Bach is often referred to as the father of Western Music. With the colossal volume of compositions written by Bach, every instrument of the Baroque era was fortunate enough to have multiple works composed for it. As is with most of his solo instrumental works, the Sonata in C Major for flute was written during Bach’s post as cantor of Leipzig’s St. Thomas’ Church in the 1720’s. This sonata is the fourth in a set of six written for the flute. I am delighted to be able to perform it for you with guitar accompaniment instead of the intended instrument, the harpsichord.

George Enescu (1881-1955)

Cantabile et Presto for Flute and Piano

George Enescu, despite being a Rumanian composer, justly deserves recognition as a contributor to the French flute repertoire. His music is just as heavily influenced by his studies with Gabriel Fauré in Paris, as by his folkloristic heritage and tzigane-like violin background. Enescu was commissioned to composer the Cantabile et Presto for Flute and Piano as the 1904 examination piece at the Conservatoire National de Paris. These pieces often shared a common structure of slow movement-fast movement to give the performer an opportunity to demonstrate their expressiveness in the lyrical opening and virtuosic dexterity in the fast second half.

Alberto Ginastera (1916-1983)

Danzas Argentinas Op. 2

Danzas Argentinas is a set of three dances for solo piano written in 1937 by Argentinian composer Alberto Ginastera. The first dance “Danza del viejo boyero” (Dance of the Old Herdsman) is bitonal, with the left hand playing only black notes, while the right plays only white notes. The second dance, “Danza de la moza donosa” (Dance of the Beautiful Maiden) is an imagery on the vastness of the Argentine grasslands. The third dance “Danza del gaucho matrero” (Dance of the Outlaw Cowboy) instructs the performer with four opening words: furiously, violently, bitingly, and wild.

Heitor Villa-Lobos (1887-1959)

Assabio a Jato (Jet Whistle)

Brazilian composer Heitor Villa-Lobos was part of a generation of Latin-American composers who created a movement to study and resurrect their county’s histories and folklores through musical language. Despite being trained as a cellist, Villa-Lobos taught himself guitar and began playing in the Rio de Janeiro scene only to quickly become an international celebrity in musical circles. Assabio a Jato, which had its premiere in New York in 1950, is saturated with sounds, effects, and melodies of the Rio Street bands, most notably at the very end of the duet when the flute creates a glissando imitating the sound of Amazonian instruments.

Program Notes

Leonard Bernstein (1918-1990)

Sonata for Clarinet and Piano

Completed in 1942, Leonard Bernstein’s Sonata for Clarinet and Piano was his first work to be published. The piece is deeply rooted to the Tanglewood Music Festival, where Bernstein met clarinetist David Oppenheim while studying conducting. The first movement of Bernstein’s Sonata, opens with a musical line that purposely hints at Hindemith, Tanglewood’s then composer-in-residence. The second movement of this two movement sonata, foreshadows Bernstein’s “West Side Story,” with time signatures exchanging between 3/8, 4/8, 5/8, 7/8 throughout creating an exhilarating effect. The premiere of this work took place at the Institute of Modern Art in Boston and the positive reception designated the work a staple of the clarinet repertoire.

George Rochberg (1918-2005)

Trio for Clarinet, Horn, and Piano

After the Second World War, the Second Viennese School, led by Serialist Americans, ran the narrative on how music was to be composed. George Rochberg shook the musical community in 1963 by rejecting the Schoenberg twelve tone system and returning to tonality. With his son’s death, Rochberg struggled to find expressions of sorrow in the twelve tone serialist system. Thus, the Trio in B-Flat Major, composed in 1947, was fortunate enough to be revised by Rochberg in 1980, after his philosophical divergence. The first movement, tagged “freely and with much expression,” features busy counterpoint and a constant, repeating interval (a 4th). The second movement, “Adagio,” is spontaneous and free, beginning with a haunting pianissimo between clarinet and muted horn. The final movement is structured around a series of rhythmic cells that eventually gives way to an animated dance.

Vincent Persichetti (1915-1987)

Parable VIII

A native of Philadelphia, Vincent Persichetti was an American composer, teacher, and pianist. Persichetti is often recognized for his contributions to wind ensemble repertoire, despite his largest single output being for piano. Persichetti’s Parable VIII is the eighth of twenty-four parables written almost entirely for solo instruments. As is with many of his works, this Parable for solo horn directly quotes a horn theme from Persichetti's own music, in this instance the seventh symphony. The parable is meant as a metaphor for the horn with the composition opening and closing with the horn fifth, the archetypal symbol for the horn.

Sergei Rachmaninov (1873-1943)

Lyric Suite

Sergei Rachmaninov was a Russian compositional leader of the late Romantic period, renowned for his virtuoso piano playing and large hands. Influenced by Tchaikovsky, Rimsky-Korsakov, and Mussorgsky, Rachmaninov’s personal style drifted toward vocal-like melodicism, lush expressiveness and rich coloration. In his piano works, Rachmaninov frequently used famous motifs, often as fragments of his opening phrase. Even in the most delicate of Rachmaninov’s music, thickness and density permeates in the harmony. The Lyric Suite is an arrangement of four of Rachmaninov’s pieces for horn and piano, a prelude, serenade, elegy, and romance. It is not clear who arranged them.

George Gershwin (1898-1937)

Three preludes

Of Gershwin's many wide-ranging compositions, the "Three Preludes," are among the very few works that were published during his lifetime. These three preludes, originally composed for piano, have been a favorite for recording artists since their publication. Born to Russian Jewish immigrants in Brooklyn, New York, Gershwin became an international sensation often appearing as soloist of his own works or in performance with his brother, Ira, who sang. Gershwin quickly developed a signature style of early 20th century French composers mixed with Jazz and Song. The “Three Preludes” are a perfect example of this mixed style. Gershwin would have been pleased to see this arrangement for clarinet and piano, the clarinet being one of his favorite instruments.
